Australia's Tourism Story

Who visits, where they go, and what they spend

Section I

Australia as a Global Destination

Australia pulled in over 8 million international visitors in 2024-25, which sounds very impressive until you realise France easily gets that in a single month. But what Australia lacks in volume, it makes up for in value. Tourists spent a record $81 billion here last year, making up nearly 3% of the whole economy.

The COVID years were brutal for tourism., to say the least. In 2020-21, revenue dropped by almost a third overnight as borders slammed shut. The bounce back since then has been genuinely surprising. By 2024-25 the industry had not just recovered but hit new highs.

Section II

Who's Visiting Australia?

New Zealand sends the most visitors, but China is where the real money comes from. Chinese tourists spent over $11 billion in Australia last year, more than any other country by a long stretch. The UK and US visitors tend to stay longer, which bumps up their spending too.

Most people come here for a holiday, but a surprisingly large number of vistors come to visit friends and family, which makes sense given how many Australians have connections overseas. Education is bigger than most people expect too, reflecting Australia's popularity as a study destination.

Section III

Where Do Tourists Go?

Without a doubt, Sydney wins. NSW gets more international visitors than any other state and Sydney alone accounted for $13.7 billion in tourist spending in 2025, up from $10.9 billion the year before. That's a huge jump in one year. Western Australia is the fastest growing though, up 23% in visitor numbers.

Outside the big cities, Queensland's Gold Coast and regional Western Australia are punching above their weight. Darwin and Hobart remain underrated destinations but are slowly building their international profiles.

Section IV

The Economic Impact of Tourism

Tourism employs 696,000 Australians which is surprisingly more than mining. The biggest employers are cafes and restaurants, followed by retail and accommodation. When COVID hit, those jobs vanished almost instantly. When tourism came back, so did the jobs which shows just how directly tied employment is to visitor numbers.

The relationship between tourism GDP and jobs is remarkably consistent over time. Both collapsed together in 2020-21 and both recovered together after borders reopened. In 2024-25, international visitors contributed $18.6 billion to tourism GDP, still growing back to their pre-COVID share of the economy.
